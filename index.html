三月是台大杜鵑花的季節，伴随的也是台北春日的疾風骤雨。去社团博览会与学系博览会，回来的路上又尽兴地淋了半身。

有人总将此处视为乌托邦与想象中的异域，有人又或者是将此视为围城。在台北的日子，越久我越会切身地觉得这两种说法的简单化与暧昧的现实的冲突。

冲突到我不知道该如何表达。

―Ｉ―
3 月。

我們像往常一樣在山路上游玩。

其中有一個最近加入我們的同伴，他的名字是 I。

是個近乎暴走族、純粹喜歡飆車的家伙。

某一天，我們的朋友 S 偷了一輛改裝成飆車用的 RZ 摩托車。

我們雖對飆車並沒有太大的興趣，但 I 顯然不同。

第二天，S一再請求，I把RZ借給了他。

接著兩天後，在我們平時一直游玩的山路上，I遭遇了事故。然後死了。

葬禮只有我們的領頭人N參加了。我們不知道要以什麼表情來面對。因此沒有去，也不能去。

那一晚，大家來到了I遭遇事故的那個轉角。

後輪鎖死的胎痕殘留，凹陷的護欄，方向指示燈的碎片散落了一地。

總是會一直聊著無聊話題的我們，在那晚也只能默然。

即使出事的轉角就在我們的眼前，像花束之類的東西，誰也沒有帶。

終於，最初把摩托車偷來的S把一根抽了一半的 Seven Star 香煙供奉在了護欄前。

其他人也都跟著做了同樣的事。

我和領頭的N並不吸煙，於是放下喝過的百威作為替代。

極冷的天氣，我們呼出的空氣化成了純白的煙霧。抬頭所望見的夜空，獵戶座顯得格外美麗。

那之後，I從我的故事中消失了。

…國道延伸，沿綫的柏青哥店建了起來，校園暴力之類的詞語開始流行…

1980 少年時代的事。

—S美—

5月

關系還算不錯的友人M撥通了我的電話。

據他說是被十分為難的事情困擾著。

那時，他大概一個星期沒來學校了，我可能有些擔心他。

M自己也不確定我能否回應他，總之就先試著找我商量一下。

“我爸沒回來。”

一開口，M就說了這樣的事情。

本來，M的家裡就沒有母親，一直處在依靠生活保障金度日的狀態。

所以M從最初就沒有依靠父母。

我們經常一起打柏青哥，我也知道他還有做著其它的工作。

雖然我覺得我們這群人可能確實有點特殊，但在我身邊的夥伴裡，從來沒有人依靠父母活著。孩子們以自己的方式獨立生活。

「……其實妹妹她……」

接下來的話便是在說M的妹妹小S美的事情。雖然低一個學年，但我還是挺熟悉她的，是個開朗可愛的孩子。

「小S美怎麼了？」

「這次雖然要退院了……」

我之前並不知情，似乎是入院了的樣子。

「是不是住院費不夠了？」

M搖了搖頭。

現在我也沒詳細了解到當時的情況，不過似乎不用擔心醫療費用的事情。

M 說的是，目前的難題是生活陷入了困境。

“確實，如果你爸不在的話，日子肯定難過……”

然而，對我的低聲自言自語，M 只是沉默。

現在回想起來，我猜 M 那時已經知道了些什麼。

3天後，退院日。

總之先找熟人安排好車輛,從稍遠的城鎮醫院把小S美接了回來。

「K同學，謝謝你。」

久違的小S美，看起來瘦了一些，

但是，羞澀時的笑臉，還是一如既往。

這時我想既然已經退了院，小S美就會很快地健康起來。

那一晚，M找我商量。總之當前的生活非常困窘。

對於從最初就不想找父母要錢的我們來說，M的話語顯露出軟弱的一面。

“我不想離開家。”

但是聽了這句話之後，我明白了很多。

不離開家的意思就是他要全職照顧妹妹，剛出院，這種情況是可以想像的。

然後，一直呆在家裡，就意味著M沒辦法去賺取生活費。

從來都是自力更生的M在這個問題上很有說服力。

第二天，我把自己所有的零錢湊在一起，准備了大約 7 萬日元，M 很高興。

六月

6月，小S美再次入院。我也坐上急救車跟了過去。

其實小S美入住的並不是普通的病房，而是臨終關懷醫院。似乎是M的老爸還在的時候就這樣子了。

因為胃癌，小S美的整個胃都被摘除掉，但最終癌細胞還是轉移了，已被告知無法醫治。

所以並不是普通的病房，而是臨終關懷醫院。

症狀緩解時，她可以回家，但病情惡化時，她會再次被送回臨終關懷病房。

可是這種反復……並不是在治病。

「不知道還可以再回幾次家……」

M這樣說道。

「小S美自己知道這事嗎？」

這個問題M並沒有回答。不過，她本人隱約知道，他補充道。

探訪病房時，像電視上一樣，S美戴著氧氣面罩呼吸著氧氣。

然後，她微微張開眼睛確認是我，又稍微有點害羞地笑了起來。

「……還好嗎？」

沒有新鮮感的問候。但那時的我，除了這些話之外什麼都說不出來。

第二天。

我在垃圾填埋場的工廠上夜班，白天M則在附近的柏青哥店打工。

互相有空的時候，我們就會去醫院，盡量不讓小S美孤身一人的作戰。

但即使如此，我還會偶爾去去學校，而M則是完全沒上過學。

不知道去了哪裡的M的老爸，依然全無歸家的樣子。

每天每天，坐在白色的牆壁前，坐在廉價的折疊椅上，與小S美聊些無關緊要的話題。

普通的病房雖然有探病時間限制，但臨終關懷醫院卻隨時可以。

我喜歡的時間，是早晨體溫檢查結束之後的８點左右。

我喜歡6月清爽的陽光，還有像是高興又像是害羞地、淺笑著的她。

兩個星期後。

小S美……不對，這時開始應該稱作S美——

第二次從臨終關懷的醫院退院了。

入院時可以用救護車，但出院時沒有這樣的待遇，我們也沒有錢打出租車。

於是我們再次請朋友開著破舊的小貨車送她回家。

然後我和M小心地把她抱上位於住宅區4樓的家。

看起來也很瘦的S美非常輕。令人伤心。

七月

那年的夏天非常炎熱。

雖然我們用風扇忍耐一下也足夠了，但擔心這會影響S美的身體。

「嗯……沒關係的。」

像往常一樣笑著回答的S美，讓我們更加難過。

連空調都沒有，我們為自身的無力感到悔恨。想要早點成爲大人。

第二天，我強行用螺絲刀和扳手拆下了我房間的空調。因爲搬家見慣了所以自己動手。

然後我和M一起把沉重的室外機抬到四樓，勉強給S美的房間裝上了。雖然雪種漏了很多，制冷效果很一般。

“哇，好涼快啊～”

盡管如此，她依然笑著說，很高興。

那天晚上，我們三個人就在制冷效果不佳的空調前慶祝了七夕。

我和M手裡拿著冰啤酒，S美則喝橙汁。雖然沒有竹枝，也沒有許願簽，卻是一個愉快的七夕。

八月

S美第三次住院。這次也是救護車送的，我不在場。

在候診室裡我和 M 聊了很久。

由於沒有父親在身邊，也沒有其他親人，所以醫院的醫生也許都是直接和 M 交談的。他們似乎決定不使用強力的化療藥物。

也許是因為肉親裡面並沒有成年人，醫生也很難判斷該如何醫治。

「大概這是最後一次了。」

M低聲說道。

他所說的“最後”，一定是指S美已經沒有回家的機會了。

假如可以回家，那麼也不會再次來到這家醫院。

白色的牆壁，廉價的折椅。高大的M佝僂著身體，就坐在那張折椅上。

2天後。

灼熱的陽光和蟬聲。

在前往醫院的路上，瀝青在熱浪中扭曲著。

我坐在折疊椅上與S美交談。

「吶，K同學……」

S美突然露出寂寞的表情。

「我，已經，不行了吶」

突然間，説出了我不想聼到的話。我想S美應該早就知道了。

但是，我不想繼續説。我不知道該如何回答，感到害怕……

真的，沒有那樣的事，很快就會好的，這樣否定掉。但是，我沒有如此堅強…

結果是我，除了默默點頭外什麼都沒有説出來。

窗外進來的灼熱光纖，讓雪白的病房變得更加蒼白。

S美發出了小小的嗚咽聲。大概我也，同樣在哭吧。

9月。

在余暑之中，S美第三次退院。

我們都很高興。本來還在擔心會不會就這樣再也不給退院了。

但恐怕，再也無法回到醫院了。M這樣說道。我也有著同樣的預感。

依然是從熟人那借了輛小四輪回家，然後兩人合力把S美抱上4樓。察覺到S美比上次又輕了一點，再次悲從中來。

在這段時間裡，偶爾會有學校的老師、志願者和來幫忙的人上門探病。這讓我稍微覺得，好心的大人還是存在著的。

到了晚上，吹著不太好用的空調，我們三人定下了一個作戰計劃。

很長的時間裏，我們雖然聊了很多，結果是，

帶S美去她想去的地方，做她想做的事情。

僅僅只是這些。就是我們這群孩子能想到的答案。

月末的夜晚

我再拜托上次的熟人，借來了車子。這回並沒有司機，只有單單一輛車。

當然，我無證駕駛了。首先，我也沒到可以考取駕照的年齡。

然後S美和M乘了進來。我操作著不太習慣的離合，深夜駕車出行。

目的地首先是不遠處的N海灘。開車15分鐘左右的距離。

然後我們，在空無一人的沙灘上放煙花。

將堆成小山的火箭和噴花煙花當成篝火點著。

我們在巨大的章魚形滑梯上玩耍。我和M互相射擊火箭煙花，雖然手上沾滿了火藥的味道，但S美笑得很開心。

而後我們三人，坐在沙灘上喝罐裝飲料。我和M是罐裝可樂，那天S美也要了橙汁。

潮水的聲音和濕潤的海風。抬頭所見的天空閃耀著夏季的星座。

波浪留下的白色泡沫痕跡，蜿蜒延伸到遠處。

一句話也沒有說，我們三人一直眺望著。

星期二 凌晨2點

那一天也和M一起留在家中。

M在裡面的房間躺著。他最近竭力照顧S美，並沒有好好休息過，所以現在該輪到我醒著了。

我覺得S美可能渴了，就給她拿了點冰塊。

然後，她輕聲地喚了我的名字。微微睜開雙眼，用微弱的聲音叫喚著。

「T同學……」

再一次叫喚我的名字吧。就一會兒，真的就要那麼一會兒……

她露出了那張一如往常的羞澀笑臉。

於是我緊緊地握住了S美的手。不知為何，就想緊緊地握著。只覺得絕對不能松開。

如此片刻。當我反應過來時，S美已經沒有了呼吸。

然後我知道了S美已經死去的事實。

她呼喚了我的名字，握住的手還溫暖著。她喜歡橙汁，她最後也給了我微笑。

就這樣，S美消失了。卻在我的故事裡刻下了一筆。

……國道延伸，沿線的柏青哥店不斷地建了起來，校園暴力之類的詞語開始流行……

1980年，少年時代的事情。

無聊的、曖昧的、冷靜的、無情的現實。

不會像電視劇和電影那樣充滿戲劇感，變化總是不被察覺，單調無趣的每一天。

明明沒有什麼值得期待的行程，卻又數著手指盼著周末的每一天。

輕視無聊的日常，尋找假想的刺激；探求容身之地，建立自身的價值觀，人人只會時常關心自己是否身處安全圈內的世界。

……但也是尚不能拋棄的世界。

那是二月，
正是隆冬時節。

少見的大雪中，
我驅車回家。

母親坐在後面的座位。

雪落高速、我茫然地想著。

恐怕是最後一次出院了。
在淀川和家往復的日子再也不會到來了……

「照顧病人真累」

是經常提起的話，
卻很難説明。
可能只有有這種經驗的人才能明白。

突然的交通事故，只留下哀傷。
像轉瞬即逝、纖細、美麗的花瓣一樣凋落。

仿佛電影般，
只會截取美麗的部分。

但以年為單位的看護，
比悲傷更多的是…

勞累。

這并不…只是薄情。

看護生命、
絕不是一件只有美麗的事情。

…誰都一定會有，
有這麽想的時候。

生命逝去的時候不是只有100%的悲傷。
心裏，也混雜了一些釋懷。

再也不用在吸烟室和臨時床鋪上熬到天明。

也不用在深夜陪同乘救護車奔波。

然後….經年累月，
内心的空洞將被填滿之時，終於能想。

幸好是生病。

幸好是能在目之所及的地方送走。

幸好不是如事故那般，突然間消失在不能見的地方。

在這並不可靠但卻曖昧的，
對誰都如此無情的世界裏…
少了一些後悔，
給予的這份“猶豫”，
是要有所感謝的。

四月。
告別儀式結束了，向照顧的人們道謝。

這是我成爲大人以來，第一次明白。
好的大人，儘管稀少還是存在的。

能爲他人著想的人也有許多。

這個世界，尚是不能抛棄的。

都立U公園。

五月。在找初到東京的工作。很快就決定了要做警備員打工。

但是，這個警備員有點奇怪。
是要警備都立公園一類的地方。

最開始完全無法理解，
主要的，是挂著巡邏的名義散步的事。

這工作一天只要繞公園四次，
然後就到管理事務所，就算只是發呆也可以。

公園本身，走一圈大概只要十分鐘左右。
像這樣一天走四圈，實在是太輕鬆了。

恐怕只是東京都，想要穿著制服的警備員，
定期的出現的狀況吧。
…就這樣，我奇怪的工作開始了。

買新宿到U站的定期票時，
尚是櫻花未落的時節。

初代警備員

我是这公园第一个保安，没有前任。

据说一年前有钓鱼的人受伤，都政府才设了这个岗。

连管理事務所的人都不清楚工作内容。

説難聼點，大概職員都是合同工，而且看起來都抱持著事不關己的態度。

正是如此，這件工作才那麽輕鬆。
早八晚五，实际散步四十分钟。日薪八千，周日和节假日休息。

散步以外的話這個工作，
就是提醒釣魚的人要注意。
這個公園的池子，是禁止用拟饵钩的，得警告钓鲈鱼的小孩。

除此之外。
真的没事可做。
总坐在长椅上画漫画。

但是無家可歸的人很多。
基本上我得到的指示是不管他們，
但看到篝火要去阻止。

無家可歸者什麽樣的人都有。
定期會出現的有自称前大公司社长的，
每天有家人来探望的，
中午就消失的。

他们的故事意外地开朗，
却裹着逞强背后的寂寞。

后来我注意到，不同於無家可歸者的人。
有一些似乎每天都會來這裏的人。

公園的住人。

一開始，有這種感覺，
是在過了一周左右。

大概是2-3人，
似乎每天都能看見同樣的面孔。

但經常見到的，其實不是2-3人。
還有更多的，
似乎每天都回來公園的人。

他們吧，年齡大小，
男性女性都有。

然後這些人，
全都是一個人。

絕對沒有幾個人聚在一起，
也沒有見過他們彼此或和他人説過話。

就如此在大白天的公園裏，沒有特別的事情，
閑逛著，特別多的人。

一開始，
我覺得是有閑暇的人。

INTRODUCTION

Background of Study

Sexual activity plays a pivotal role in romantic relationships, yet research on its impact in young adults remains limited. Sharon Sassler and Fenaba R. Addo’s (2012) study on 600 low-to-moderate-income couples revealed that over one-third engaged in early sexual activity, correlating with lower relationship quality, especially for women. Brian J. Willoughby and Dean M. Busby’s (2014) separate study supported this conclusion, indicating a decrease in the quality of relationships with early sexual involvement.

Norval Glenn (2003) highlighted the emotional complexities of early sexual involvement, making it challenging to end unhealthy relationships. S. K. Whitbourne (2012) suggested that early sexual activity may foster commitment, leading to the persistence of suboptimal relationships.

Jason S. Carroll’s (2014) perspective emphasizes the importance of early sexual behavior in testing sexual compatibility for long-term relationships. However, previous studies, characterized by sample homogeneity and small sizes, may limit the detection of significant relationships between variables.

Cultural differences, as explored by Silvia Ubillos, Darío Paez, and José Luis González (2000), contribute to variations in sexual behavior. The study revealed associations between social and cultural variables, religious aspects, and sexual permissiveness. Moreover, socio-economic development and cultural dimensions play a crucial role in sexual frequency, challenging stereotypes about Christianism and oriental religions. This research provides valuable insights into the complex interplay of early sexual activity, cultural influences, and relationship dynamics.

Significance of study:
Today, characterized by evolving relationship dynamics, premature sexual behavior in young adult relationships is prevalent. Approximately 50% of young couples engage in sexual activity before commitment (Sassler & Kamp Dush, 2009). Despite this trend, there is limited research on early sex. This study contributes to understanding modern romantic relationships, offering insights into evolving concepts of love, interpersonal relationships, and mental health. The findings may inform comprehensive sex education, interventions, and policies addressing sexually transmitted diseases and societal issues.

Research questions and Hypothesis
Research questions How does sex before or early in a relationship affect the intimate relationships of the younger generation?

Hypothesis
Main hypothesis: There is a negative relationship between early sex and the quality as well as length of intimate relationships.
Sub-hypothesis #1: People with different cultural backgrounds are influenced differently in the length and quality of the relationship from the sex before or early in relationship.
Sub-hypothesis #2: Having sex before or early in a relationship can establish trust and intimacy immediately.
Sub-hypothesis #3: People having early sex before a relationship is established have a higher chance of cheating.

METHOD

Participants:
Approximately 100 individuals aged 18 to 35 from diverse backgrounds (Eastern & Western countries) will be recruited for our study on the impact of early sexual activity in intimate relationships. Out of these, 65 will participate in the survey, and 5 will be selected for interviews. Participants must have experienced sex in a relationship, with a distribution of 60% having had sex before the relationship and 40% not.

Recruitment:
A mixed-method recruitment approach will be employed, combining social media and traditional methods. Flyers and physical questionnaires will be distributed in schools, and online surveys will be shared through a dedicated page link. Social media platforms will be utilized, emphasizing hashtags related to youth, relationships, and early sex. Recruitment materials will highlight the voluntary and confidential nature of participation, eligibility criteria, and researcher contact information.And for interview, acquaintances with relevant experiences or knowledge in psychology and social behavior will be selected for interviews. Incentives will be provided to encourage questionnaire completers to participate in interviews.

Measures

Measurement of Independent Variables (IV):

Early Sex: We assess the timing of early sexual activity by asking participants about the occurrence of sexual activities within one week of establishing a relationship.

Cultural Background: To capture participants’ cultural influences, we utilize questions from established scales such as the Multigroup Ethnic Identity Measure (MEIM) and Cultural Orientation Scale (COS) by Bierbrauer, Meyer, and Wolfradt.

Measurement of Dependent Variables (DV) and Relationship Features:

Quality and Length of Romantic Relationship: Using specific questions from the Dyadic Adjustment Scale (DAS) by Graham Spanier, we measure the quality and duration of romantic relationships. Opening questions explore relationship dynamics, focusing on factors influencing length.

Quick Trust and Intimacy: We gauge trust and intimacy through questions from the Experiences in Close Relationships Scale (ECR) by Brennan, Clark, and Shaver. Adjustments are made to dimensions of avoidance and anxiety, with a focus on early relationship stages to minimize recall bias.

Cheating: Likelihood of infidelity is measured using questions from the Infidelity Questionnaire by Blow and Hartnett, allowing exploration of the potential for cheating in romantic relationships.

Data Collection Procedures
We employ a dual approach, utilizing a questionnaire survey and in-depth interviews. The survey, consisting of 15-20 questions, takes approximately 10 minutes for participants to complete anonymously on a website. This questionnaire will be divided into sections. And participants have the option to express interest in an in-depth interview. Interviews, conducted in a semi-structured format, align with the questionnaire outline, focusing on characteristics influenced by sexual behavior since relationship inception. And we will focus on the special outcome of the interviewees in questionnaire.
After survey and interviews, we meticulously structured and cleaned the data, categorizing participants into two groups based on early sexual behavior (1 for early sex, 0 for no early sex) for comparison. For those engaging in early sex, we analyzed cultural background scores, categorizing them by cultural region if scores were “moderately correlated” or higher.
Next, we analyzed questionnaire Section 2 scores on relationship quality, length, trust, intimacy, and cheating separately for both groups. Correlation analysis and statistical tests (t-tests or ANOVA) were applied to test main hypotheses and Hypotheses 2 and 3, ensuring accuracy.
Data visualization methods, including tables and bar charts, were used to compare the impact of early sex on relationship quality and length across different cultures, validating Hypothesis 1.
For opening questions, a thematic analysis identified recurring themes and patterns. Keywords were extracted and frequency counted to examine the relationship between early sex and relationship length commitment.
Interview transcripts were re-recorded and organized. Thematic analysis explored responses’ depth, revising the survey-based model based on interview insights.

DISCUSSION

For the quality of relationship. Groups that have engaged in early sex have far more regrets and arguments in their relationships than those that have not. This is because, due to the “sexual chemistry” reaction is short-term temporary, so after a period of time after the incompatibility of both parties will be exposed, this kind of feelings do not have a long-term basis for accumulation, so both parties are prone to feelings of regret. Surprisingly, in terms of future expectations, those who continued to have early sex had higher expectations of the future because they viewed sex as a manifestation of high development stage of the relationship. Their own early involvement in sex suggests that they are more satisfied with each other in terms of physical attraction. This leads to higher levels of satisfaction with physical contact.

The challenge in sustaining relationships with early sexual behavior stems from several factors. Primarily, such unions rely heavily on passion, bypassing crucial stages of intimacy and trust development, making them prone to disputes and eventual dissolution. Engaging in early sexual behavior often raises expectations, magnifying negative aspects upon reality’s arrival. Despite assumptions of swift termination, these relationships persist due to societal and individual views of sex as a commitment, leading to endurance to avoid guilt or societal judgment. Post-coital experiences may foster unrealistic fantasies, obscuring potential compatibility issues. Influenced by the moment’s intensity, individuals may inaccurately perceive compatibility. Nevertheless, lacking careful consideration, these relationships succumb to inherent deficiencies, highlighting their transient nature.

Empirical data supports sub-hypothesis#1, indicating that early sexual behavior significantly affects romantic relationships, especially in East Asian societies and to a lesser extent in other cultures. East Asian societies, more family-oriented, devalue future romantic connections linked to early sex, subjecting love relationships to strict social control. This societal influence fosters reluctance to end relationships, resulting in gradual deterioration. In the Middle East, influenced by religious conservatism like East Asia, relationship quality suffers from cultural and social controls despite the absence of early sexual engagement. European and American cultures, with lenient moral norms, do not prolong troubled relationships due to early sexual behavior, and romantic progress remains unaffected. South Asian and Latin American cultures, with an intermediate cultural climate, have fewer social limitations regarding sex compared to other cultures.
For sub-hypothesis#2, early sexual behavior in a relationship does not necessarily foster quick trust and intimacy. While commitment is crucial, sexual commitment differs from overall relationship commitment. Rapid sexual development may cause neglect of other relationship aspects, hindering emotional depth and communication and the trust as well as intimacy are based on time accumulated. Quick progression might lead to a perception that feelings are solely passion-driven, raising suspicions about loyalty, and diminishing trust between partners.

Sub-hypothesis 3 establishes a significant link between early sexual engagement in relationships and a higher likelihood of infidelity. This correlation is attributed to reduced guilt and increased susceptibility to “emotional” motivations in those engaging in early sex. The accelerated onset of early sexual behavior may lead to a lack of mutual awareness, revealing value disparities that spark conflicts and potential relationship deviations. Individuals engaging in early sexual behavior may be driven by a heightened interest in sex, a diminished sense of responsibility, and a perception of sex as pleasure rather than a commitment expression, making them more prone to novel experiences. Alternatively, lower satisfaction and stability, combined with inadequate knowledge of partners, may elevate dissatisfaction and incline individuals towards extramarital affairs.

Conclusion

This study examined the effects of early sexual behavior on the quality and stability of romantic relationships. The results showed that early sexual behavior was associated with lower relationship satisfaction, higher conflict, lower trust, higher infidelity, and higher breakup rates. These effects varied across different cultural contexts, with East Asian cultures being more negatively affected than others. The study suggested that early sexual behavior might hinder the development of intimacy and commitment, and increase the risk of relationship dissolution.
Enlightens:
The study advocates reducing early sexual engagement, emphasizing the distinction between sexual and relationship commitment. In other words, we should also balance the physical attraction with emotional connection in Relationships. Despite discouraging early sex, it calls for a nuanced perspective on sexuality in relationships, highlighting the need to address cultural biases and societal perceptions to safeguard relationship integrity.
Study limitations
The study’s scope, conducted in Hong Kong, may have been influenced by its cultural context, limiting generalizability. The focus on East Asian culture could introduce bias, and gender differences were not adequately considered in the sub-hypotheses’ statistical analyses. Future research should address these limitations for a more comprehensive understanding.

The Essay
In this course , what I focus is the religious of Taiwan. And based on this topic , I wrote the summary essay about one key view “ rituals are public but faith is private”.

In Taiwan, the distinction between rituals and faith is a key characteristic of the religious landscape. For example, Taiwanese folk religion and Buddhism often involve elaborate public rituals and processions, such as the Mazu pilgrimage, where entire communities come together to participate. These rituals serve to reaffirm social bonds and collective identity, rather than being solely focused on individual spiritual growth. The material aspects of the rituals, such as ritual objects and liturgical procedures, play a significant role in attracting and engaging participants, even those without a strong personal faith.

On the other hand, religious faith in Taiwan tends to be more private and personal. Individuals may develop their own understandings and relationships with the divine, independent of the public rituals. This allows for a diversity of belief systems and spiritual practices to coexist within the broader framework of Taiwanese religious culture. The public nature of rituals does not necessarily translate into a shared or uniform set of religious beliefs among the participants.

This distinction between the public and private dimensions of religion highlights the complex and multifaceted nature of religious experience in Taiwan, where collective identity and individual spirituality are not always neatly aligned.

The public-private dichotomy in Taiwanese religion has profoundly shaped the development and expression of faith within the region, fostering a vibrant religious landscape that is both diverse and cohesive. Public rituals, such as temple festivals and religious processions, act as platforms for expressing shared values and traditions, transcending individual differences in faith. These communal activities embody Émile Durkheim’s concept of “collective effervescence,” strengthening communal bonds and providing a material and performative engagement for participants. For instance, during the annual Mazu pilgrimage, thousands of devotees join in the procession, regardless of their personal beliefs, creating a powerful sense of unity and shared identity.

In contrast, the private nature of religious belief in Taiwan facilitates a high degree of pluralism and syncretism. Individuals freely draw from various spiritual traditions, embodying Max Weber’s “value-rational” mode of action. The lack of a dominant institutional authority enables this fluidity of religious identity. For example, many Taiwanese may simultaneously venerate Mazu, a sea goddess, and participate in Humanistic Buddhist practices, such as those promoted by the Four Great Mountains, without seeing any contradiction. This syncretism is not just a blending of teachings but a merging of practices, allowing individuals to craft a personalized spiritual path.

From a “political/religious” perspective, the separation between public rituals and private beliefs ensures that religious activities do not directly engage in political affairs. This distinction allows for a coexistence of multiple religious identities within a single community, which can be seen in the way local government supports cultural heritage policies that preserve religious rituals without endorsing any particular belief system. An example of this is the recognition of the Dajia Jenn Lann Temple’s pilgrimage as a cultural heritage event, which celebrates the ritual aspect while respecting the diverse personal beliefs of participants.

Regarding the “secular/sacred” divide, the public nature of rituals means they are often integrated into the fabric of daily life and community events. The secularization of religious practices, such as the transformation of religious festivals into tourist attractions, blurs the lines between the sacred and the secular. For instance, the Baishatun Mazu pilgrimage has evolved into a significant tourist event, with visitors participating in the procession for reasons ranging from spiritual devotion to cultural curiosity. This culturalization of religion has allowed traditional rituals to persist in modern society, as evidenced by the increasing participation of non-believers in religious processions, drawn by the unique cultural experiences and the material textures of the rituals.

Thus, the public-private distinction in Taiwanese religion not only enriches the religious landscape but also contributes to the social harmony and cultural diversity of the region, allowing for the coexistence of public rituals and individualized beliefs without a governing mechanism on belief.

Contemporary trends in Taiwan show a shift toward belief-based religions among younger generations, moving away from traditional ritual-based practices. This change is driven by several factors: the alignment of belief-based religions with contemporary values, emphasizing personal spiritual experiences and “value-rational” expressions; the globalization of religious ideas, which challenges traditional practices; and the vulnerability of public rituals to political and social influences, which diminishes their appeal. These dynamics encourage younger individuals to seek more personalized and spiritually centered religious experiences.

Despite this shift, the public-private distinction continues to enrich the overall religious landscape in Taiwan. The coexistence of ritual-based and belief-based traditions fosters a vibrant ecosystem of religious expression, where the material, the social, and the spiritual intersect in complex and dynamic ways. This diversity allows Taiwanese religion to adapt to the changing demands of its people, contributing to the resilience and relevance of faith in the modern era.

While the distinction between public rituals and private faith is a useful heuristic for understanding the religious dynamics in Taiwan, it may not fully capture the nuances and complexities of individual and collective religious experiences.

Firstly, the boundary between the public and private realms is not always clear-cut. Individuals may participate in public rituals as a means of expressing their personal faith, and private beliefs can also inform one’s engagement with communal religious activities. The interplay between the two dimensions is often more fluid and multifaceted than the binary framework suggests.

Additionally, the emphasis on material and performative aspects of public rituals may overlook the deeper spiritual and emotional resonances that participants may experience. Even those drawn to the aesthetic or social dimensions of rituals may develop a sense of personal faith and connection to the divine over time.

Today, I pick up the topic of The Formation of Residents’ Local Identity through the of Mazu pilgrimages. And my opinion on this is the community and local identity based on Mazu pilgrimages is fluid.

First, let’s consider the concept of the worship circle. In traditional Taiwanese society, the worship circle, is a group of people who share a common area of worship and often contribute to the upkeep of a temple. An example of this can be seen in the community surrounding the Dajia Mazu Temple in Taichung.

residents of Dajia Town participate in the annual Mazu procession, contributing financially and volunteering their time. However, the composition of the worship circle is not static; it exhibits a degree of fluidity that allows for the inclusion of individuals who may not reside in Dajia but still maintain a strong connection to the temple and the community. For instance, former residents who have moved away often return to participate in the procession, bringing with them new perspectives and resources. And also brings more persons who related to them into the worship.

Moving on to the organization of pilgrimage groups, we observe different methods of grouping participants. Taking the Baishatun Mazu Pilgrimage as an Example,” involves a range of participants, including residents of Baishatun, immigrants returning to participate, and newcomers who have joined after experiencing the pilgrimage. These groups form based on village affiliation, surnames, inter-village alliances, and even online communities. For example, the residents of Baishatun and the neighboring villages of Beizhuang and Nan Zhuang form the core of the pilgrimage. Additionally, individuals from distant places, such as Taipei, have joined the pilgrimage and contributed to its organization, blurring the lines between local and non-local participants.

Another interesting aspect is the role of volunteer service communities along the pilgrimage route. These groups provide essential support to pilgrims. For instance North T’ou (北投) area, with its strong tourism industry, has attracted pilgrims and volunteers who offer hospitality and support. local residents and businesses provide refreshments and rest areas for the pilgrims. This voluntary service reinforces the fluid nature of boundaries within the religious community.

According to these examples, we can got the point that the communities identification’s structured are floating . And we can simply classify the community takes part into the pilgrimage into four type. Territorial Community/Homeland Community/ Religious Community /Imagined Community
1. [Territorial Community: This refers to the area centered around the temple where the goddess is enshrined. Despite the erosion of territorial significance, the residents of Baishatun and nearby villages continue to participate in the procession, maintaining a sense of belonging to the local community.
2. Homeland Community: This community links immigrants with residents based on the notion of homeland and relationships involving kinship, property, and religious activities. Immigrants who return to participate in the procession often maintain strong ties with their place of origin, despite living elsewhere.
3. Religious Community: This includes all Mazu believers who share the common experience and religious sentiments of the pilgrimage journey. Believers from different backgrounds assert a sense of belonging to the Mazu cult and claim a communal identity. However, this identity is constructed in diverse situations, allowing for distinctions between “we-group” and “they-group.”
4. Imagined Community: This can be formed through films of the pilgrimage and communication via internet discussion boards, where the common sentiment and experience of the pilgrimage journey is always asserted.]

And these four community actually also have overlapping and floating boundary, For instance, the residents of Xinpu Village in Nanzhuang Township, who had a dispute with the Baishatun community, no longer participate in the official territorial worship but many still personally believe in and worship Baishatun Mazu. They can choose to participate in the procession on an individual basis, illustrating the flexibility of boundaries.

Now, why can Taiwanese folk religion exhibit such fluid boundaries? To answer this question, we can turn to the sociological concept of liminality, as introduced by Victor Turner. Turner describes liminality as a transitional state where the normal boundaries of society are temporarily suspended. During Mazu pilgrimages, the usual social structures are set aside, creating a sense of unity and equality among participants. This liminal space allows for the formation of temporary communities that transcend traditional boundaries, fostering a sense of collective identity and shared experience.

Moreover, the work of Max Weber can help us understand the dynamics at play. Weber saw religion as a force that shapes societal norms and values. In the case of Mazu pilgrimages, the shared religious practice acts as a unifying factor that brings together people from diverse backgrounds. The fluidity of boundaries in these religious gatherings reflects Weber’s notion of a religious community that transcends the physical and social limitations of everyday life.
The fluid boundaries of Mazu pilgrimages significantly enhance social cohesion and community resilience. These pilgrimages foster unity and shared purpose by allowing individuals from diverse backgrounds to participate flexibly. This inclusivity strengthens social bonds and encourages cooperation, even among those who do not reside in the same geographical area.
The adaptability of pilgrimage groups to include diverse participants reflects a societal shift towards inclusivity and adaptability. Popular religion as part of life has its resilience. In a rapidly changing world, the Mazu pilgrimage exemplifies how traditional practices can evolve to remain relevant, engaging younger generations and new participants through social media and online communities.
The pilgrimage’s fluid boundaries also help bridge the urban-rural divide, a significant issue in contemporary Taiwan. Urban residents who return to their hometowns to participate in the pilgrimage reconnect with their roots, fostering a sense of belonging and responsibility towards their place of origin.
While fluid boundaries allow for modern elements and diverse participants, they also preserve cultural heritage. The pilgrimage acts as a living tradition, continuously practiced and passed down through generations.
